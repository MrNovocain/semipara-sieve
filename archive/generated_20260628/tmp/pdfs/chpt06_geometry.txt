2.2   covariance & correlation
With standard deviation to measure variability, suppose we consider the
variability in the sum of two random variables, X+Y . It is natural to ask
if the variability in such a sum is either more or less than the variability in
either one of them separately. The answer, it turns out (not very surpris-

In passing, consider briefly a rather curious, and almost pathological, counter
example to the theorem when the measure µ is not -finite. Counting mea-
sure, ⌘, on the real line is defined on the set of all subsets, P(R) ⇠= 2R , by

And, of course, this measure, as presented, may certainly be restricted only
to the borel subsets of the real line if we so choose. But it is perfectly clear
that, by its very definition, the only null-set/zero-set of this measure is the
empty set, ?, itself. And for this reason, alone, any other measure defined

to it! This is harsh indeed! And, of course, the Radon-Nikodym theorem
